Case Study · New Optical Palace

Building a $1M+ B2B Pipeline Across 35 Accounts

New Optical Palace · Canada · 2018–2025

The Situation

Managing a B2B account portfolio in Canada across 35+ accounts sounds straightforward on paper. In reality, it means juggling 35 different relationships, 35 different sets of expectations, 35 different buying cycles — and trying to grow revenue across all of them simultaneously while keeping every client satisfied enough to stay and expand.

At New Optical Palace, I inherited a portfolio that had potential but lacked structure. Accounts were managed reactively. There was no systematic approach to identifying upsell opportunities, no clear framework for prioritising which relationships needed attention and when.

The Challenge

The core challenge was one of scale and focus. With 35+ accounts, you can't give everyone equal attention — but you also can't afford to neglect anyone long enough for them to start looking at alternatives. The question was how to create a system that kept all accounts moving forward while concentrating the highest-value effort on the highest-value opportunities.

At the same time, the Canadian market had its own dynamics — relationship-driven purchasing decisions, longer evaluation cycles and a strong emphasis on reliability and consistency over price. The playbook I'd developed in India needed meaningful adaptation.

The Approach

I started by segmenting the portfolio — not just by revenue, but by growth potential and relationship health. This gave me three clear tiers: accounts to protect and deepen, accounts to actively grow and accounts to stabilise before they became at-risk.

For the growth tier, I built structured discovery conversations into every quarterly touchpoint — not to pitch, but to genuinely understand how their business had evolved and where our offering could create more value. This surfaced upsell and cross-sell opportunities that would never have emerged from a standard renewal conversation.

I also introduced personalised outreach cadences for each account — relevant industry news, useful resources, occasional check-ins with no commercial agenda. In the Canadian market, this kind of consistent, low-pressure presence built the kind of trust that made commercial conversations significantly easier when the time came.

On the prospecting side, I used structured discovery calls and consultative engagement to convert new B2B accounts, always leading with understanding their operational challenges before introducing solutions.

What I Learned

Managing a large account portfolio well is ultimately a prioritisation problem. The temptation is to treat all accounts equally — the reality is that strategic focus on the right accounts at the right time drives disproportionate results. The system matters as much as the relationships.

The best account managers I've seen don't just manage relationships — they architect them. They know exactly where each account is headed and what needs to happen to get it there.

The key takeaway

Portfolio management at scale requires both a systematic approach and genuine human connection. The system tells you where to look; the relationship determines what you find when you get there.
